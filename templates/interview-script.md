# Customer Discovery Interview Script Template

A 30-minute structured interview for validating problem and solution assumptions. Generated during the gaps stage (07) when primary research is needed for a specific idea validation.

Customize all `[bracketed]` sections for the specific idea being validated.

```markdown

# Customer Discovery Interview: [Idea Title]

## Before the Interview

### Logistics
- Record the conversation (with permission)
- Have a note-taker if possible, or use transcription
- Prepare to listen more than talk — aim for 80/20 (them/you)

### Mindset
- You are here to learn, not to pitch
- Disconfirming evidence is more valuable than confirmation
- One real story is worth ten opinions about hypotheticals

### Key Assumptions to Test

1. [Assumption 1 — e.g., "Mid-market SaaS companies lose significant revenue to poor onboarding"]
2. [Assumption 2 — e.g., "Current onboarding tools require too much manual configuration"]
3. [Assumption 3 — e.g., "Teams would pay $X/month for an automated solution"]

---

## Interview Structure

### Opening (3 minutes)

> Thanks for taking the time. I'm exploring [problem space — NOT your solution] and trying to understand how people like you deal with it today. There are no right or wrong answers — I'm just trying to learn from your experience. Nothing is being sold here.
>
> Do you mind if I record this so I don't miss anything? The recording is just for my notes.

**Key:** Establish that this is about THEIR experience. Do not mention your idea yet.

---

### Part 1: Context & Role (3 minutes)

Goal: Understand their world before diving into the problem.

1. Can you tell me about your role and what your team is responsible for?
2. What does a typical [week/sprint/quarter] look like for you?
3. [Context question specific to the problem area — e.g., "How does your team handle new customer onboarding today?"]

**Listen for:** Their language for the problem. Their priorities. Where [problem area] ranks in their daily concerns.

---

### Part 2: Problem Exploration (10 minutes)

Goal: Understand the problem from their perspective. Go deep on real experiences, not hypotheticals.

4. Walk me through the last time [problem situation occurred — e.g., "you onboarded a new customer"]. What happened?
5. What was the hardest part of that?
6. How often does [problem] come up? [Weekly? Monthly? Every new customer?]
7. When [problem] happens, what is the impact? [On you personally? On the team? On the customer? On revenue?]
8. What have you tried to solve or work around this? What happened?

**Follow-up prompts:**
- "Tell me more about that."
- "What happened next?"
- "Why was that particularly frustrating?"
- "Can you give me a specific example?"

**Listen for:** Emotional language (frustrated, painful, annoying) = real problem. Bland language (it's fine, we manage) = low severity. Specific dollar amounts or time costs = strong signal.

---

### Part 3: Current Alternatives (7 minutes)

Goal: Understand what they use today and where the gaps are.

9. What tools or processes do you currently use for [problem area]?
10. What do you like about your current approach?
11. What is missing or broken about it?
12. Have you looked at other solutions? What happened? [If they tried and rejected something, this is gold — understand why.]
13. How much time [or money] does your team spend on [problem area] per [week/month]?

**Listen for:** Whether they are actively looking for solutions (strong signal) vs. passively tolerating the problem (weaker signal). Switching costs and lock-in with current tools.

---

### Part 4: Solution Reaction (5 minutes)

**Only go here if you have strong problem validation from Parts 2-3.** If the problem seems weak, stay in exploration mode.

> Based on what I'm hearing, I've been thinking about [brief, plain-language description of the solution concept — 2 sentences max, no jargon, no feature list].

14. What is your initial reaction to that?
15. What would need to be true for something like that to be useful to you?
16. What concerns would you have?
17. [If relevant] What would you expect to pay for something like that? [Or: How would you compare that to what you spend on [current approach] today?]

**Key rules:**
- Describe the concept, not the product. No screenshots, no demos, no feature lists.
- Do NOT defend or explain further if they push back. Say "That's really helpful, tell me more about that concern."
- Their objections are data, not obstacles to overcome.

---

### Closing (2 minutes)

18. Is there anything about [problem area] that I should have asked about but didn't?
19. Who else on your team [or in your network] deals with this? Would you be open to connecting me? [Referral ask — do this every time.]
20. Can I follow up with you as this evolves?

> This was incredibly helpful. Thank you for being so open about your experience.
```

## Customization Notes

When generating this template for a specific validation:

1. **Replace all [bracketed] content** with specifics from the idea and its assumption map
2. **Tailor Part 2 questions** to the specific problem being validated — use the language from the problem statement
3. **Tailor Part 3 questions** to known competitors from the competitive analysis
4. **Adjust Part 4 solution description** based on the solution fit section — keep it conceptual, not feature-level
5. **Select 2-3 assumptions** from the assumption map that are Critical + Weak/None evidence — these are the interview's primary targets
